Disability representation in Korean films

2020 Fall Data Analysis & Visualization project
mts.team@snu

People with disabilities make up 5% of the Korean population.*
However, out of the 1,080 of films that have been released from 2000 to 2020,
only a small number have characters with disabilities.**

38

We analyzed 38 characters with disabilities in 28 of these films
to explore the current state of disability representation in Korean films.

Gender

We assigned the gender of the characters based on official film information and on how they were referred to throughout the film.

The gender distribution favors the appearance of male characters, their number 35% more than those of female characters.

Hover over the images for more information about the character.

Age

Age was also based on official film information and whether it was mentioned in the film. In cases where it is unclear, it was based on the actor's age at the time of filming.

When it comes to age distribution, the group of characters in their 20s was the largest. No characters in their 50s or older appeared.

Hover over the images for more information about the character.

Disability

The most common group represented was people with hearing impairments, followed by people with intellectual disabilities, physical disabilities, ASD, and visual impairments. People with language disorders and brain lesions had the fewest appearances in their respective films.

Hover over the images for more information about the character.

Result

We were able to identify estimated appearance rates of both characters with and
without disabilities, as well as the different emotions expressed throughout the films.

In whole: Different patterns according to gender and age

In the case of female disabled characters with a high appearance ratio, most were physically impaired. Their age was heavily concentrated around 30. On the other hand, male characters were composed of a mixture of physical & mental impairments, and the age range was more diverse than among women.

Romance: Man falls in love with disabled woman

In most romance movies, heroines were physically impaired. Their male counterparts usually did not have disabilities and appeared more, which can be seen in parts of the story where they attempt to win the female characterâ€™s heart by looking for them or trying to gain their attention. For both genders, there were no mentally impaired characters that appeared in the romance genre. The main character and supporting actor can be clearly distinguished by their appearance rates.

Comedy: Story of a mentally impaired man and his family

Many disabled male characters are mentally impaired, and they commonly have 'helper' characters (e.g. brothers) who have higher appearance ratios than them. There were no female characters with disabilities represented in comedy movies. Instead, female characters present do not have disabilities and are either characters looked after (e.g. daughters) or relied on (e.g. mothers) by male characters with disabilities.

Emotional diversity of disabled characters

Characters with hearing impairments were found to have expressed the most amount of sadness, fear, and surprise, while those with intellectual disabilities were shown to have portrayed the most expressions of happiness and disgust.

Discussion

We propose a discussion about the implications of misrepresentations regarding
1) movie characters and real-world statistics, 2) acting patterns, and 3) algorithm andg
dataset biases on the exploration of disability representations in films.

Misrepresentation of the Ratio

Compared to the distribution of disabilities in the real world***, there are major differences in the ratio. For example, people with physical disabilities make up nearly 50% of the total disabled people in Korea, but there were only 17% of them among the 38 movie characters. 1% of registered disabled people had autism, but there were 9% of the movie characters with the disability.This result shows that some disabilities are under/overrepresented in movies. We assume that this is because certain disability types are 'chosen' more for the plot instead of representing the natural ratio.

Acting like 'the disabled'

As we have seen emotion detection mistakes in the previous section, we discuss two assumptions.

Firstly, this might have occurred due to acting performances. As non-disabled actors portrayed all 38 disabled characters, there is a chance that their acting patterns ('acting like the disabled') are biased. This bias may have intervened in the process and affected the detected emotion results.

"They feel disgust when they smile"

Secondly, we can assume that there exists a bias in the emotion detection algorithm itself. On the right, there is a face detected as 'disgust', but with a very high smile value and consecutive images detected as 'happy'. We have found similar patterns particularly among mentally impaired characters.

This may be because of how disabled people are underrepresented in the algorithm training dataset. Unlike many discussions being made about gender and racial bias, disability bias in datasets and its negative influence are barely mentioned and recognized.
